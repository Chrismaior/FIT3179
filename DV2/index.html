Global Crime Overview

Crime is not evenly spread across the globe, it reflects the pressures and opportunities within each society. This choropleth map highlights the regions that contain the most crime around the world. We therefore see that Latin America carries some of the world’s highest crime burdens, driven by inequality, drug trade, and urban violence. South Africa also shows persistently high levels, linked to social and economic challenges. In contrast, Europe, East Asia, and parts of Oceania remain among the safer regions, supported by stronger institutions and stability. These contrasts remind us that crime is shaped by deeper patterns of governance, opportunity, and community resilience, rather than being a universal experience.

Time Trends in Crime

Victims of Intentional Homicide, 1990–2018

Looking at how crime has evolved over time, intentional homicide shows a clear global arc: many countries reached their highest levels in the early–mid 1990s, followed by a long downward shift through the 2000s, with stabilization or modest decline by 2018. This pattern is not uniform, as Latin American nations such as El Salvador, Colombia, and Honduras display persistently high and volatile rates, while Europe and much of Asia trend lower and steadier. By comparing single countries or pairs, the chart highlights how social, political, and economic contexts shape safety outcomes over time. Intentional homicide was chosen because, as the treemap above demonstrates, it is the dominant crime category across all regions. To enhance exploration, the chart includes interactive controls: a country selector to compare different national contexts, a time slider to zoom in on specific periods, and a toggle for a 5-year moving average that smooths year-to-year fluctuations and highlights the underlying trend. Looking at its time trends provides the most direct lens into how societies succeed (or struggle) in improving public security.
