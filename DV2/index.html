Mapping the Geography of Crime

Crime is not evenly spread across the globe, it reflects the pressures and opportunities within each society. This choropleth map highlights the regions that contain the most crime around the world. We therefore see that Latin America carries some of the world’s highest crime burdens, driven by inequality, drug trade, and urban violence. South Africa also shows persistently high levels, linked to social and economic challenges. In contrast, Europe, East Asia, and parts of Oceania remain among the safer regions, supported by stronger institutions and stability. These contrasts remind us that crime is shaped by deeper patterns of governance, opportunity, and community resilience, rather than being a universal experience.

Continental breakdown in Crime Types

The Types of Crime charts highlight how regions face different crime pressures. Europe and Asia carry the highest burden of drug offences, while the Americas stand out for homicides, reflecting deep-rooted violence and organised crime. Economic and corruption crimes dominate in Europe, showing systemic governance challenges. Meanwhile, violent and sexual crimes are spread across regions, reminding us these issues cut across borders. Together, these patterns show that crime is shaped not just by numbers, but by inequality, governance, and enforcement capacity. This breakdown shows how tackling crime requires cooperation and tailored strategies.

Worldwide Crime Rate Comparison

Click on continent label for more in depth regional view

This treemap represents the absolute total number of reported crimes by category over 20 years for each region around the world. It reveals a clear imbalance in the types of crime that shape regional safety challenges . In both Europe and the Americas, intentional homicide dominates the picture, overshadowing other forms of crime such as sexual violence or theft. This suggests that both regions face a common struggle with lethal violence. In Asia, intentional homicide again stands out, but alongside smaller shares of sexual exploitation and other offenses, hinting at broader social issues that coexist with violent crime.

Taken together, the treemap suggests that intentional homicide is not just another category of crime, it is a recurring and defining challenge across regions. Given the strong persistence of intentional homicide, we will look further into how that has developed over time in the next section. By pairing this treemap with the Time Trends in Crime chart, users can move from seeing where homicide dominates to exploring how it has changed over time. With interactive tools like the moving average and time slider, the dashboard allows us to uncover whether countries are successfully reducing violence, or if homicide remains an entrenched issue.

Decades of Change in Global Crime

Victims of Intentional Homicide, 1990–2018

The boxplot below shows how, crime rates fluctuate between 2019 and 2023, across the five continents. Africa showed low and fairly stable levels with mild peaks in 2020 and 2022, while the Americas had the highest and most volatile rates, reflecting strong disparities across countries. Asia and Europe displayed moderate peaks around 2020–2022 before declining in 2023, suggesting gradual improvement. Oceania saw the sharpest drop, from high variability in 2019–2020 to minimal levels by 2023, hinting at effective interventions or post-pandemic recovery. Together, these observations highlight that progress in public safety is not linear but closely tied to how societies adapt to evolving challenges over time. The trends highlight how governance and reform speed shape safety outcomes.

Beyond the numerical shifts, these patterns reveal how crime is deeply intertwined with broader social and economic transitions. Regions experiencing political stability and improved governance tend to show sustained reductions in homicide, whereas nations facing inequality, corruption, or weak institutions often struggle with volatility. Post-2020 changes further reflect the global impacts of the pandemic, where disrupted economies, shifting law enforcement priorities, and social stressors temporarily altered crime trajectories. Regional differences show uneven progress, reflecting unique policy and social dynamics. Overall, the charts indicate a general global decline in crime rates after 2020, though regional differences remain pronounced.

Looking at how crime has evolved over time, intentional homicide shows a clear global arc: many countries reached their highest levels in the early–mid 1990s, followed by a long downward shift through the 2000s, with stabilization or modest decline by 2018. This pattern is not uniform, as Latin American nations such as El Salvador, Colombia, and Honduras display persistently high and volatile rates, while Europe and much of Asia trend lower and steadier. By comparing single countries or pairs, the chart highlights how social, political, and economic contexts shape safety outcomes over time. Intentional homicide was chosen because, as the treemap above demonstrates, it is the dominant crime category across all regions. To enhance exploration, the chart includes interactive controls: a country selector to compare different national contexts, a time slider to zoom in on specific periods, and a toggle for a 5-year moving average that smooths year-to-year fluctuations and highlights the underlying trend. Looking at its time trends provides the most direct lens into how societies work in improving public security. In many ways, these shifts reflect broader transformations, from peace agreements to changing economic opportunities and law enforcement strategies. Understanding these trends helps contextualize today’s crime patterns and the progress, or setbacks, made in public safety.
Understanding the Forces Behind Crime

Between 2021 and 2023, global resilience to organised crime showed small yet uneven gains across regions. The bar chart above shows improvements in international cooperation (+0.19 to 5.87) and national policies and laws (+0.06 to 5.48) reflect stronger cross-border partnerships and renewed legislative focus on combating transnational crime. These advances point to a growing global consensus on the need for coordinated frameworks and shared accountability. However, progress in governance (4.70, −0.03) and transparency (4.36, −0.05) continues to decline, exposing vulnerabilities in political integrity and institutional oversight. Many nations continue to rely on security

-focused responses such as law enforcement (4.87, −0.04) and territorial integrity (5.06, −0.05), while social and community-based resilience efforts remain underdeveloped. Civic indicators like victim and witness support (4.24) and non-state actor engagement (4.72, −0.16) remain the weakest globally, signalling limited trust between governments and citizens. Strengthening anti-money laundering (+0.02 to 4.71) and prevention (+0.08 to 4.55) initiatives has shown promise, but sustainable resilience will depend on balancing enforcement. Overall, progress remains limited — true resilience depends on pairing enforcement with stronger governance, integrity, and community participation.
